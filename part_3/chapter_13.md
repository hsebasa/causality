## Chapter 13. The Translation Theorem

**13.1 Definition (measured layer).** Add $(U, \Sigma, \mu)$, standard Borel; mechanisms measurable. **Lemma:** solution maps, hence every $Y(\iota)$, are measurable.

**13.2 Theorem (Translation: the Rubin bridge, both directions).** *($\Rightarrow$)* The schedule under $\mu$ is a stochastic process $\{Y(\iota)\}_\iota$ on the *single* space $(U, \mu)$ — **the missing probability space of the potential-outcomes framework is the unit space** — with consistency and SUTVA holding pointwise (10.2–10.3), hence surely; observed data $(W, Y(W))$ is the consistency-diagonal pushforward. *($\Leftarrow$)* Every consistent finite-valued family of potential-outcome random variables arises this way: take $U$ to be the response-type space with $\mu$ the family's law — the response-function representation (Balke–Pearl lineage [verify]). Deterministic core $+$ census $=$ stochastic potential outcomes: an equivalence, cheap in both directions. *Proof:* App. C.

**13.3 Definition (FFRCISTG, relocated).** The schedule precedes $\mu$; per-setting independence is a property of $\mu$ over it: for every complete setting $v^\dagger$, the evaluations $\{f_v(pa^\dagger_v, \cdot)\}_v$ are independent under $\mu$. Product noise ($\mu = \bigotimes_v \mu_v$, NPSEM-IE) is strictly stronger — the canonical example, not the standing assumption. **Seam:** each unit reveals one world (11.1); the measure is the *license to let other units stand in for the worlds that did not run*.

**13.4 Example (Markov chains: a particular case).** A discrete-time Markov chain — a state set with a stochastic matrix $P(x,y)$ — is, read against the book, a **graded law at the affine-stochastic face** (5.2; 6.2's matrix dictionary with normalized rows), carrying the **trivial splitting and the empty frame**. Everything chain-theoretic is an instance: Chapman–Kolmogorov is matrix composition; $n$-step transitions are $f^n$; the Markov property is *automatic*, since states are complete descriptions (3.1) — a "non-Markov" process is one whose declared state is too coarse, repaired by the unrolling/history construction (1.18), which *is* Markovianization; recurrence/transience classification is the measured refinement of the $\mathbf{2}$-shadow ($\rho$-shapes, 5.3). What a chain *lacks* is everything causal: no splitting, so no parents or mechanisms (Ch. 8); no frame, so no surgery — a chain can be conditioned but not intervened on from inside its formalism (Ch. 9); no units, so no schedule, no $Y(\iota)(u)$ (Ch. 10). **A Markov chain is a measured causal structure with everything causal switched off**; Parts I–II are the inventory of what must be added before a kernel deserves to be called a causal model.

**13.5 Construction (continuous-time Markov chains, derived).** Apply the flow definition (6.5–6.6) at the affine face: a family of stochastic matrices $\{P_t\}_{t \geq 0}$ with $P_0 = I$ and $P_s P_t = P_{s+t}$ — Chapman–Kolmogorov *is* the flow law. This is exactly a continuous-time Markov chain. Two presentations, instancing Generated Equivalence (6.4): *(i) Rate matrix:* a declared $Q$ (off-diagonal rates $\geq 0$, zero row sums) presents the flow as $P_t = e^{tQ}$ (finite case). $Q$ is **differential** structure (App. A) — a derivative, not a member of the family — consistent with the no-atomic-step theorem: divisible time still has no smallest tick. *(ii) Uniformization — the law returns:* for $\lambda \geq \max_x |q_{xx}|$, $\Pi := I + Q/\lambda$ is a *discrete* stochastic matrix, and $P_t = \sum_n e^{-\lambda t} \tfrac{(\lambda t)^n}{n!} \Pi^{\,n}$: **a CTMC is a discrete law run at the ticks of a Poisson clock** — continuous time as law $+$ randomized clock, with exponential holding times as the atom-level face of the flow property. Qualitative shadow: for every $t > 0$, $\mathrm{supp}(P_t)$ is full reachability in the rate graph — the $\mathbf{2}$-shadow of a CTMC is *scale-free*, the dense-future phenomenon of 4.3 [verify: CTMC support dichotomy].
