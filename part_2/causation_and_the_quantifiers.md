## Causation and the Quantifiers

*A standalone interlude, depending on Ch. 1 and the adjoint of Ch. 2. In the pointful semantics (Def. 1.4) a first-order predicate $B(x)$ over a domain $D$ is a family $\{B(d)\}_{d \in D}$ of events; the quantifiers are $\forall x\,B(x) := \bigwedge_{d} B(d)$ and $\exists x\,B(x) := \bigvee_{d} B(d)$. Write $A \sqsubseteq B$ for causation (1.11′). The single axiom (join-preservation, Def. 1.5) settles how $\sqsubseteq$ meets the quantifiers.*

**§1 Proposition (existential — valid).** *If $B(d) \sqsubseteq C(d)$ for every $d \in D$, then $\exists x\,B(x) \sqsubseteq \exists x\,C(x)$.* *Proof.* $\bigvee_d C(d) \le \bigvee_d f^{\ast}B(d) = f^{\ast}\big(\bigvee_d B(d)\big)$, the equality by join-preservation of $f^{\ast}$. $\square$

**§2 Proposition (universal — invalid).** *$B(d) \sqsubseteq C(d)$ for every $d$ does **not** imply $\forall x\,B(x) \sqsubseteq \forall x\,C(x)$.* *Minimal example.* $X = \{p, q, r, z\}$, sharp law $f(p)=p$, $f(q)=f(r)=z$, $f(z)=z$; domain $D = \{1, 2\}$ with $B(1)=\{p,q\}$, $B(2)=\{p,r\}$, $C(1)=C(2)=\{z\}$. Each instance holds: $z \in f^{\ast}\{p,q\}$ and $z \in f^{\ast}\{p,r\}$. But $\forall x\,B(x) = \{p\}$ (a genuine possibility) and $\forall x\,C(x) = \{z\}$, while $z \notin f^{\ast}\{p\} = \{p\}$. $\square$ The two instances are witnessed by *different* states ($q$ and $r$); $\forall$ keeps only their shared antecedent $\{p\}$, which is inert.

**§3 Remark (why, and the dual).** Join-preservation gives §1; meets are not preserved (only $f^{\ast}(\bigwedge S) \le \bigwedge f^{\ast}S$, 1.6c), which is exactly the gap in §2 — and $\forall$ does pass through when the law merges nothing (a sharp $f(A)=g[A]$ preserves meets iff $g$ is injective). Dually, the right adjoint $f^{\sharp}$ of Ch. 2 *preserves* meets, so abduction commutes with $\forall$: $f^{\sharp}(\forall x\,C(x)) = \forall x\,f^{\sharp}(C(x))$. **Production is existential; explanation is universal.** (Internalized as a connective on events in Ch. 7, where this same asymmetry reappears as the cause-slot behaviour of $\triangleright$.)
