## The Category of Causal Structures

*A standalone interlude, depending only on Chapter 1. Chapter 1 fixed one law $(L, f)$ at a time; here we let laws talk to one another. Objects are causal structures (Def. 1.5); a morphism is the bare structure-preserving map between them — the join-preserving square that intertwines two laws. Items are numbered §1–§5 to keep them separate from the chapter ($1.x$) and appendix ($A$–$F$) schemes. $\mathbf{Sup}$ denotes the category of complete lattices (Def. 1.3) and arbitrary-join-preserving maps; $\circ$ is composition of underlying functions.*

**§1 Definition (morphism of causal structures).** Let $(L, f)$ and $(L', f')$ be causal structures (Def. 1.5). A **morphism** $h : (L, f) \to (L', f')$ is a map $h : L \to L'$ such that, for **every** index set $I$ and family $(A_i)_{i \in I}$ in $L$:
$$\text{(join-preservation)}\quad h\Big( \bigvee_{i \in I} A_i \Big) = \bigvee_{i \in I} h(A_i), \qquad\qquad \text{(equivariance)}\quad h \circ f = f' \circ h.$$
The second condition says the square
$$\begin{array}{ccc} L & \xrightarrow{\ f\ } & L \\[2pt] {\scriptstyle h}\big\downarrow & & \big\downarrow{\scriptstyle h} \\[2pt] L' & \xrightarrow{\ f'\ } & L' \end{array}$$
commutes. **Consequences.** (a) Taking $I = \varnothing$ in join-preservation gives $h(\bot) = \bot'$: a morphism preserves the impossible event (1.6a in the codomain matches the empty join). (b) $h$ is monotone: $A \leq B \Rightarrow h(B) = h(A \vee B) = h(A) \vee h(B) \geq h(A)$ — the argument of 1.6b, now for $h$. (c) Nothing is asked of meets or of $\top$: a morphism need not preserve either (it is a $\mathbf{Sup}$-map, not a lattice-with-top map). The equivariance clause is exactly the relation already written for simulations in 1.18(iii) ($\pi \circ \hat f = f \circ \pi$) and in 15.1 ($h \circ f_E = f_H \circ h$); what is new here is isolating it, together with join-preservation, as the bare arrow — stripped of the observability and intervention data that Chapter 15 will later load onto it.

**§2 Definition (identity; composition).** *(Identity.)* For a causal structure $(L, f)$, the identity map $\mathrm{id}_L : L \to L$ preserves all joins and satisfies $\mathrm{id}_L \circ f = f = f \circ \mathrm{id}_L$; it is therefore a morphism $\mathrm{id}_{(L,f)} : (L, f) \to (L, f)$. *(Composition.)* Given morphisms $h : (L, f) \to (L', f')$ and $k : (L', f') \to (L'', f'')$, their composite is the underlying set-function composite $k \circ h : L \to L''$.

**§3 Proposition (the data is well-defined).** *Identities are morphisms, and the composite of two morphisms is a morphism.* *Proof.* Identities are handled in §2. Let $h, k$ be as in §2. Join-preservation composes: for any family $(A_i)_{i \in I}$,
$$(k \circ h)\Big( \bigvee_i A_i \Big) = k\Big( \bigvee_i h(A_i) \Big) = \bigvee_i k(h(A_i)) = \bigvee_i (k \circ h)(A_i),$$
using join-preservation of $h$ then of $k$. Equivariance composes:
$$(k \circ h) \circ f = k \circ (h \circ f) = k \circ (f' \circ h) = (k \circ f') \circ h = (f'' \circ k) \circ h = f'' \circ (k \circ h),$$
using associativity of function composition and equivariance of $h$ then of $k$. $\square$

**§4 Definition + Theorem (the category $\mathbf{Caus}$).** Let $\mathbf{Caus}$ be the following data: its **objects** are the causal structures $(L, f)$ of Def. 1.5; its **morphisms** are the maps of §1; **composition** and **identities** are those of §2. **Theorem.** *$\mathbf{Caus}$ is a category.* *Proof.* Composition lands in morphisms and identities are morphisms by §3, so the operations are defined on the stated hom-sets. Because morphisms compose as underlying functions, the two remaining axioms are inherited from $\mathbf{Set}$: **associativity** $(\ell \circ k) \circ h = \ell \circ (k \circ h)$ for composable $h, k, \ell$, and the **unit laws** $h \circ \mathrm{id}_{(L,f)} = h = \mathrm{id}_{(L',f')} \circ h$. $\square$ **Structural reading.** An object of $\mathbf{Caus}$ is exactly an *endomorphism in $\mathbf{Sup}$* (a complete lattice carrying a join-preserving self-map), and a $\mathbf{Caus}$-morphism is exactly a commuting square of such endomorphisms in $\mathbf{Sup}$. So $\mathbf{Caus}$ is the arrow-style category of $\mathbf{Sup}$-endomaps: the law $f$ is the object's distinguished arrow, and a morphism transports one law to another.

**§5 Proposition + Remark (isomorphisms; forgetful functor; tie to Chapter 15).** **Proposition (isomorphisms).** *A morphism $h : (L, f) \to (L', f')$ is an isomorphism in $\mathbf{Caus}$ iff $h$ is a bijection whose set-inverse $h^{-1}$ is itself join-preserving — equivalently, $h$ is an isomorphism in $\mathbf{Sup}$.* *Proof.* If $h$ has a $\mathbf{Caus}$-inverse it is in particular a bijection with join-preserving inverse. Conversely, if $h$ is a $\mathbf{Sup}$-isomorphism, then composing $h \circ f = f' \circ h$ with $h^{-1}$ on both sides gives $h^{-1} \circ f' = f \circ h^{-1}$, so $h^{-1}$ is equivariant and hence a morphism $(L', f') \to (L, f)$; the two composites are identities. $\square$ **Remark (forgetful functor).** Forgetting the law gives a functor $U : \mathbf{Caus} \to \mathbf{Sup}$, $(L, f) \mapsto L$ and $h \mapsto h$; it is faithful (a morphism is determined by its underlying $\mathbf{Sup}$-map) and reflects nothing of $f$ beyond equivariance. Its fiber over a fixed carrier $L$ — all laws on one event structure — is itself a complete lattice and a quantale, developed in the interlude *The Algebra of Laws* (after Ch. 2). **Remark (Chapter 15).** A *simulation* in the sense of 15.1 is a $\mathbf{Caus}$-morphism enriched with two further data — an observability pullback $h^{-1}(\mathcal{O}_H) \subseteq \mathcal{O}_E$ and matched intervention repertoires ($h \circ g = g' \circ h$). The bare $\mathbf{Caus}$-morphism defined here is its skeleton; the Pullback Transfer theorem (15.1) — equal latencies, reachability transported — is precisely what the equivariance clause of §1 buys, before any observation or intervention structure is added. An isomorphism in $\mathbf{Caus}$ is the "total two-way transfer" tier of that theorem's three-tier grammar.
