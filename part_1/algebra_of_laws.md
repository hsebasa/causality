## The Algebra of Laws

*A standalone interlude, depending on Chapter 1 and on Chapter 2 (the adjoint 2.1–2.2 and the graph dictionary 2.3); it is placed after Chapter 2 accordingly. Chapter 1 fixes one law at a time, and the categorical interlude compares laws across different carriers; here the carrier is held fixed and the laws themselves are collected into a single object. On one event structure $L$, the maps satisfying the axiom form an algebra whose operations are the ways mechanisms combine — superposition, consensus, sequencing — and that algebra is an event structure in its own right, carrying causal structure one level up (§8) ; §9 identifies $\mathbf{Caus}$ itself as a category of modules over the algebra the law freely generates, and §10 reads off the module dictionary — each world's own clock face among its entries. Nothing here extends the object language: the single-law accounting of Ch. 7 is untouched; every construction lives at the level of structures. Items are numbered §1–§10.*

**§1 Definition + Theorem (the lattice of laws).** For an event structure $L$ (Def. 1.4) let $\mathrm{Law}(L)$ be the set of maps $f : L \to L$ satisfying the one axiom (Def. 1.5 — preservation of arbitrary joins), ordered **pointwise**: $f \leq g :\iff f(A) \leq g(A)$ for every $A$. Reading: every production $f$ asserts, $g$ also asserts — $f$ is a *sub-mechanism* of $g$. **Theorem.** *$\mathrm{Law}(L)$ is closed under pointwise joins of arbitrary families — $(\bigvee_i f_i)(A) := \bigvee_i f_i(A)$ is again a law, and is the least upper bound — so $\mathrm{Law}(L)$ is a complete lattice.* *Proof.* $(\bigvee_i f_i)(\bigvee_j A_j) = \bigvee_i \bigvee_j f_i(A_j) = \bigvee_j (\bigvee_i f_i)(A_j)$ — joins commute with joins; the pointwise join is clearly the least pointwise upper bound; meets then come free (Lemma 1.3). $\square$ Its $\bot$ is the inert law $A \mapsto \bot$; its $\top$ sends every nonzero event to $\top$ (and $\bot$ to $\bot$, as 1.6a forces any law to): "anything possible produces everything." The book's derived operators are elements: every power $f^{n}$ (1.8) and the cones $f^{\ast}$ (1.14) and $f^{+}$ (7.1) are pointwise joins of laws, hence laws. (In the language of the categorical interlude: $\mathrm{Law}(L) = \mathbf{Sup}(L, L)$, and its elements are exactly the objects of $\mathbf{Caus}$ with carrier $L$ — the fiber of the forgetful functor over $L$, §5 there.)

**§2 Proposition (meets exist, but not pointwise).** *The pointwise formula $m(A) := f(A) \wedge g(A)$ is monotone but can fail the axiom; the meet in $\mathrm{Law}(L)$ is instead $f \wedge g = \bigvee \{ h \in \mathrm{Law}(L) : h \leq f,\ h \leq g \}$ (Lemma 1.3 in $\mathrm{Law}(L)$), which lies below the pointwise candidate, in general strictly.* *Witness.* $X = \{a, a', b, c\}$, $R_f = \{(a,b), (a',c)\}$, $R_g = \{(a,c), (a',b)\}$: then $m(\{a\}) = \{b\} \wedge \{c\} = \bot = m(\{a'\})$ yet $m(\{a, a'\}) = \{b,c\} \wedge \{c,b\} = \{b,c\}$ — not a join — while every law $h$ below both has $h = \bot$ on all atoms, so $f \wedge g = \bot$. $\square$ The two mechanisms agree on no single production; they only *appear* to agree on the coarse event $\{a, a'\}$, whose two conjuncts are witnessed by *different* atoms — the merging that breaks $\forall$ (*Causation and the Quantifiers* §2) and the cause-slot meet (7.4c), one level up. Reading: **consensus of mechanisms cannot be computed event-by-event.**

**§3 Theorem (canonical semantics: causal graphs union and intersect).** *For $L = \mathcal{P}(X)$ the dictionary of 2.3 is an order-isomorphism $\mathcal{P}(X \times X) \cong \mathrm{Law}(\mathcal{P}(X))$, $R \mapsto f_R$; consequently $f_R \vee f_S = f_{R \cup S}$ — join is **union of causal graphs** — and $f_R \wedge f_S = f_{R \cap S}$ — meet is **intersection of causal graphs**.* *Proof.* $R \subseteq S \iff f_R \leq f_S$ (test on atoms, 2.3), and the correspondence is onto (2.3 again); an order-isomorphism transports all joins and meets; $(R \cup S)[A] = R[A] \cup S[A]$ shows the join is even pointwise, while $f_{R \cap S}\{x\} = R(x) \cap S(x)$ shows the meet is pointwise **on atoms only**, then freely join-extended. $\square$ §2's witness relocated: $R_f \cap R_g = \varnothing$, so the meet is $\bot$ exactly as computed — the overshoot of the pointwise formula happens only at coarse events. Reading: $f \vee g$ places two mechanisms **side by side**; $f \wedge g$ keeps the productions **both assert**.

**§4 Theorem (the quantale of laws).** *Composition of laws is a law, and it distributes over arbitrary joins in both slots:*
$$\Big(\bigvee_i f_i\Big) \circ g \;=\; \bigvee_i (f_i \circ g), \qquad\qquad f \circ \Big(\bigvee_i g_i\Big) \;=\; \bigvee_i (f \circ g_i),$$
*the second **because** $f$ preserves joins — the axiom is what makes sequencing linear. Hence $(\mathrm{Law}(L), \bigvee, \circ, \mathrm{id})$ is a unital quantale: the structure of Def. 6.1 minus commutativity.* *Proof.* Left equation: evaluate pointwise. Right: $f(\bigvee_i g_i(A)) = \bigvee_i f(g_i(A))$. Associativity and unit are function composition's. $\square$ Non-commutativity is genuine: $R_f = \{(a,b)\}$, $R_g = \{(b,c)\}$ give $(g \circ f)\{a\} = \{c\}$ but $(f \circ g)\{a\} = \bot$ — *sequence matters*. On graphs, composition is relational composition ($R_{g \circ f} = R_f \mathbin{;} R_g$ — the "$;$" of G.1, in diagrammatic order); at $L = V^{X}$ the $V$-matrices of 6.2–6.3 (the $\mathbf{Laws}$ of 6.4a) form the module-endomorphism subquantale, matrix join and $\otimes$ being $\vee$ and $\circ$ in coordinates, the two coinciding at $V = \mathbf{2}$ (3.2b). Being a quantale, $\mathrm{Law}(L)$ has residuals — division of mechanisms — but these are not postulated: they are Theorem 2.1 applied one level up, and are identified in §8(c). Contrast of readings: the commutative quantales of Ch. 6 grade *connection* (value objects); the law quantale composes *mechanism* — one algebra of what runs, not of what it is worth.

**§5 Theorem (the star of a join: interleaving, synergy, monotonicity).** *(a) $(f \vee g)^{\ast} = \bigvee \{\, w : w$ a finite word in $f, g \,\}$ — reachability in a joined world is the join over all **interleavings** — and $f^{\ast} \vee g^{\ast} \leq (f \vee g)^{\ast}$, in general strictly. (b) The star is monotone: $f \leq g \Rightarrow f^{\ast} \leq g^{\ast}$.* *Proof.* (a) Expand $(f \vee g)^{n}$ by §4's two-sided distributivity into the join of all length-$n$ words; join over $n$ (1.14); the one-letter words give the inclusion. Strictness: $R_f = \{(a,b)\}$, $R_g = \{(b,c)\}$ — the word "$f$ then $g$" puts $c \in (f \vee g)^{\ast}\{a\}$, while $f^{\ast}\{a\} = \{a,b\}$ and $g^{\ast}\{a\} = \{a\}$. (b) $f^{n} \leq g^{n}$ by induction (monotonicity 1.6b at each step); join. $\square$ **Synergy.** Mechanisms in parallel produce what neither produces alone; every route through a joined world alternates contributions. **One-signedness.** Deleting a mechanism never adds reachability — sub-mechanism, sub-star, by (b) — so removal moves the world in one direction only: the frame of axiom-removal surgery (21.2) is monotonicity of the star read as surgery. **Combination breaks AC.** $R_f = \{(a,b)\}$ and $R_g = \{(b,a)\}$ each satisfy AC (4.2); their join is the 2-cycle. Likewise $R_g = \{(a,b), (c,d)\}$ and $R_f = \{(b,c), (d,a)\}$ are separately acyclic while $f \circ g$ contains the 2-cycle $a \to c \to a$. **Feedback is a property of the ensemble, never of a single mechanism** — the dependency graphs of 11.1 acquire their cycles only in combination. **The signature, located.** The operations assembled in §§1, 4–5 — $(\bot, \mathrm{id}, \vee, \circ, {}^{\ast})$ — are exactly the signature of Theorem G.1: Kozen completeness is the equational theory of this algebra on canonical carriers, and the meet of §2 is deliberately absent from that signature. Equationally the interleaving description collapses to the denesting law $(f \vee g)^{\ast} = (f^{\ast} \circ g^{\ast})^{\ast}$, derivable from G.1's axioms.

**§6 Proposition (stability panel) + Proposition (branching as superposition).** Over the canonical semantics (where each property is graph-theoretic), which world properties survive which operations — the instrument-panel question (2.8) asked of the algebra:

| property | $\vee$ | $\wedge$ | $\circ$ |
|---|---|---|---|
| the axiom (lawhood) | stable (§1) | stable (§2) | stable (§4) |
| sharpness (2.3) | fails — branching | fails — halting | stable |
| AC (4.2) | fails (§5) | stable | fails (§5) |
| no-merging (meets forward, 2.8) | fails | stable | stable |
| forward-closure of a region (1.9) | stable | stable | stable |

*Witnesses and one-line proofs.* Two distinct sharp laws branch under $\vee$ (their union offers a menu) and halt under $\wedge$ (empty successor set wherever they disagree — totality lost); composites of functions are functions. AC: §5's two witnesses; under $\wedge$, subgraphs cannot create cycles. No-merging: $R_f = \{(a,c)\}$, $R_g = \{(b,c)\}$ are separately injective and merge jointly; subgraphs only shrink successor sets, and composites of meet-preserving laws preserve meets. Traps: $(f \vee g)(Z) = f(Z) \vee g(Z) \leq Z$; $(f \wedge g)(Z) \leq f(Z) \leq Z$; $(f \circ g)(Z) = f(g(Z)) \leq Z$ — **invariants survive the entire algebra**: whatever region each mechanism respects, every combination respects. $\square$ **Proposition (branching = superposed determinism).** *With choice, every total relational law is the join of the sharp laws below it: $f_R = \bigvee \{ f_s : s$ a selection through $R \}$.* *Proof.* Each $(x, y) \in R$ lies in some selection (choose $y$ at $x$, arbitrarily elsewhere — totality permits); the union of the selections is $R$. $\square$ The menu of 2.5(ii) as an equation: **nondeterminism is a join of determinisms** — the lattice-side face of the hidden-coordinate resolution 2.6(b).

**§7 Theorem (the adjoint mirror).** *Abduction reverses the whole algebra: for laws $f, g, f_i$,*
$$\Big(\bigvee_i f_i\Big)^{\sharp} = \bigwedge_i f_i^{\sharp} \ \ (\text{pointwise}), \qquad (f \circ g)^{\sharp} = g^{\sharp} \circ f^{\sharp}, \qquad f \leq g \ \Rightarrow\ g^{\sharp} \leq f^{\sharp}.$$
*Proof.* $C \leq (\bigvee_i f_i)^{\sharp}(B) \iff \bigvee_i f_i(C) \leq B \iff \forall i:\ C \leq f_i^{\sharp}(B)$ — the adjunction (2.1), three times. $C \leq g^{\sharp}(f^{\sharp}(B)) \iff f(g(C)) \leq B \iff C \leq (f \circ g)^{\sharp}(B)$. Antitonicity: $f(C) \leq g(C) \leq B$. $\square$ The right-hand meet **is** pointwise: meet-preserving maps (2.2) are closed under pointwise meets, dually to §1 — production and explanation trade the failure. For meets of laws only the lax $f^{\sharp} \vee g^{\sharp} \leq (f \wedge g)^{\sharp}$ survives. By the dual of 2.1 every arbitrary-meet-preserving endomap has a (join-preserving) left adjoint, so $f \mapsto f^{\sharp}$ is a bijection onto the meet-preserving endomaps — an order-**anti**-isomorphism converting joins to pointwise meets and reversing composition. Reading: **combining mechanisms disjunctively intersects their explanations** — the largest event guaranteed to produce within $B$ under *either* mechanism must be so guaranteed under *both*; production is existential, explanation universal (2.2, *Causation and the Quantifiers* §3), here as a mirror of algebras.

**§8 Theorem (the algebra is itself an arena) + honesty clause.** *$\mathrm{Law}(L)$ is an event structure (Def. 1.4) whose events are mechanisms — and it carries causal structure. (a) **Translations are laws on laws:** for every $f \in \mathrm{Law}(L)$, $\Lambda_f(g) := f \circ g$ and $\mathrm{P}_f(g) := g \circ f$ preserve arbitrary joins, so $(\mathrm{Law}(L), \Lambda_f)$ and $(\mathrm{Law}(L), \mathrm{P}_f)$ are causal structures; they commute ($\Lambda_f \circ \mathrm{P}_g = \mathrm{P}_g \circ \Lambda_f$), and every $g \mapsto \bigvee_i f_i \circ g \circ f_i'$ is likewise a law on laws. (b) **Chapter 1 recurs one level up:** $\Lambda_f^{\,n}(g) = f^{n} \circ g$, so $\Lambda_f^{\ast}(g) = f^{\ast} \circ g$ — the future of a mechanism is "that mechanism, then anything the world can do" — and in particular $\Lambda_f^{\ast}(\mathrm{id}) = f^{\ast}$: **the star of $f$ is the future cone of "doing nothing" in the algebra of laws** (1.9 re-run on $\mathrm{Law}(L)$ returns 1.14). Moreover, for every $A \in L$, evaluation $\mathrm{ev}_A(g) := g(A)$ is a morphism $(\mathrm{Law}(L), \Lambda_f) \to (L, f)$ of causal structures (categorical interlude §1): the mechanism-level world shadows the ground world along every event at once. (c) **Chapter 2 recurs: residuals are abduction.** Theorem 2.1 applied to the laws $\Lambda_f, \mathrm{P}_f$ on the complete lattice $\mathrm{Law}(L)$ yields right adjoints $\Lambda_f^{\sharp}(h) = \bigvee\{ g : f \circ g \leq h \}$ and $\mathrm{P}_f^{\sharp}(h) = \bigvee\{ g : g \circ f \leq h \}$ — exactly the two residuals of the non-commutative quantale (the division of 6.1, one-sided): the largest mechanism whose every composite with $f$ stays inside $h$. Abduction of mechanisms is division of mechanisms, by the same theorem that gave abduction of events.* *Proof.* (a) Linearity of both slots (§4); commutation is associativity. (b) Induction on $n$, then the pointwise join (1.14); $\mathrm{ev}_A$ preserves joins by the pointwise order, and $\mathrm{ev}_A(\Lambda_f(g)) = f(g(A)) = f(\mathrm{ev}_A(g))$ is equivariance. (c) 2.1 applies because $\Lambda_f, \mathrm{P}_f$ preserve joins; unwind the adjunction. $\square$ **Honesty clause (no canonical law without a declaration).** The parameter-free candidates fail the axiom. Self-application $g \mapsto g \circ g$ is bilinear, not linear: with $R_f = \{(a,b)\}$, $R_g = \{(b,c)\}$, $(f \vee g) \circ (f \vee g)$ sends $\{a\}$ to $\{c\}$ while $(f \circ f) \vee (g \circ g)$ sends it to $\bot$. The star $g \mapsto g^{\ast}$ is monotone — Scott-continuous, even — but not join-preserving: §5's strictness *is* the failure; it is a closure operator, not a law (the characteristic failure of 1.21iii, one level up; no tension with 6.4's reflection, whose codomain is the *spaces* with their own joins — reflect after joining, which is §5's denesting law again). So the algebra of laws is an event structure on which causal questions open only once a law is **declared** — the stance of 1.22, applied by the theory to its own machinery; and states that carry their own law — the regime-change reading that 7.7(c) and 8.10 leave outside the one-law scope — would take exactly such a declaration, on a carrier built from $\mathrm{Law}(L)$; Ch. 10 builds it. **The tower.** $f \mapsto \Lambda_f$ preserves joins, composition, and the unit ($\Lambda_{f \vee f'} = \Lambda_f \vee \Lambda_{f'}$ by §4; $\Lambda_{f \circ f'} = \Lambda_f \circ \Lambda_{f'}$; $\Lambda_{\mathrm{id}} = \mathrm{id}$) and is injective ($\Lambda_f(\mathrm{id}) = f$): each level of laws embeds in the next by translation, $\mathrm{Law}(L) \hookrightarrow \mathrm{Law}(\mathrm{Law}(L)) \hookrightarrow \cdots$ — the construction never closes.

**§9 Theorem (the time algebra; causal structures are modules).** Let $T := (\mathcal{P}(\mathbb{N}), \subseteq, +, \{0\})$: joins are unions, the tensor is the **Minkowski sum** $S + S' := \{ m + n : m \in S,\ n \in S' \}$, the unit is $\{0\}$ — *menus of latencies*, with "or" as union and "and then" as sumset; equivalently the language quantale $\mathcal{P}(\Sigma^{\ast})$ of 6.1 over a one-letter alphabet, commutative for exactly that reason. *(a) Freeness.* For every unital quantale $Q$ and every $q \in Q$ there is exactly one quantale morphism $h : T \to Q$ with $h(\{1\}) = q$, namely $h(S) = \bigvee_{n \in S} q^{n}$. *Proof.* Unit and tensor force $h(\{n\}) = q^{n}$, joins force the formula; the formula is a morphism by distributivity. $\square$ *(b) The canonical action.* Apply (a) to $Q = \mathrm{Law}(L)$ (§4) and $q = f$: every causal structure carries the **derived** action
$$S \cdot A \;:=\; \bigvee_{n \in S} f^{n}(A),$$
no declaration anywhere — the universal property makes every choice. The book's operators are scalars: $f^{\ast} = \mathbb{N} \cdot (-)$ (1.14), $f^{+} = \mathbb{N}_{\geq 1} \cdot (-)$ (7.1), $\mathsf{F}_{n} = \{n\} \cdot (-)$ (7.8); reachability is the action of $\{1\}^{\ast} = \mathbb{N}$. *(c) The latency-set space.* On states (2.3), $d(x, y) := \{\, n : y \in f^{n}(\{x\}) \,\}$ satisfies $\{0\} \subseteq d(x, x)$ and $d(x, y) + d(y, z) \subseteq d(x, z)$ — a $T$-valued causal space (6.1), derived. Its images along quantale morphisms are the temporal rows of 6.1's table: the support $\sigma : T \to \mathbf{2}$ gives reachability $\rightsquigarrow$ (4.1); $\min$ gives the latency $\tau$ (1.16) and the quasi-metric of 5.1; the mod-$p$ quotients give phases, and cycles are diagonal facts ($d(x,x) \cap \mathbb{N}_{\geq 1} \neq \varnothing$, 4.5). The non-temporal rows — strength, capacity, labelled routes — are *not* quotients of $T$: they carry information the bare law lacks, and enter only by declared base change (6.4). **Theorem.** *$\mathbf{Caus}$ is isomorphic to $T\text{-}\mathbf{Mod}(\mathbf{Sup})$: the category of complete lattices carrying a unital, associative action of $T$ that preserves joins in each slot, with join-preserving action-equivariant maps.* *Proof.* (b) makes every causal structure a module. Conversely an action is determined by its generator: $f := \{1\} \cdot (-)$ is join-preserving; $\{n\} = \{1\} + \cdots + \{1\}$ forces $\{n\} \cdot (-) = f^{n}$ and joins force $S \cdot (-) = \bigvee_{n \in S} f^{n}(-)$; the assignments are mutually inverse. A module map commutes in particular with $\{1\} \cdot (-)$ — the equivariance square of the categorical interlude §1 — and equivariance at the generator propagates to the whole action by induction and joins. $\square$ **Reading.** This is the $k[x]$ fact of linear algebra, one shelf over: a $k[x]$-module is a vector space with one chosen operator; a $T$-module is an event structure with one law. *The law is a scalar; time is the free scalar algebra; dynamics is module theory.* Meets appear nowhere — the tensor lives on the production side, as it must (§7). \***Dividends.** The graded case reads $V\text{-}\mathbf{Caus} \cong V[x]\text{-}\mathbf{Mod}$ for the power-series quantale $V[x]$ (maps $\mathbb{N} \to V$ under convolution; $\mathbf{2}[x] = T$), so Ch. 6's base change $\mathbf{2} \to V$ is change of scalars $T \to V[x]$; flows (6.5) are modules over $\mathcal{P}(\mathbb{R}_{\geq 0})$, relocating the divisibility watershed (App. A) into the scalar algebra; and the forgetful $U$ (categorical interlude §5) acquires a left adjoint, the free module $T \otimes (-)$ — over a state set the free causal structure is $\mathcal{P}(\mathbb{N} \times X)$ with law "advance the stamp": the carrier of the unrolling (1.18), before the world's step is written on it.

**§10 Definition + Proposition (the effective time algebra; the module dictionary).** Write $T_{f} \subseteq \mathrm{Law}(L)$ for the image of §9's canonical morphism — the commutative subquantale $\{\, \bigvee_{n \in S} f^{n} : S \subseteq \mathbb{N} \,\}$ of all scalars: **the algebra of pure time as this world experiences it**, the $\mathbf{Sup}$-analogue of the polynomials $k[M]$ in a single matrix inside the full operator algebra (§4). Isomorphic worlds have isomorphic clock faces: a $\mathbf{Caus}$-isomorphism conjugates $T_{f}$ onto $T_{f'}$ (conjugation preserves pointwise joins and composition). **Proposition (4.3, restated as the shape of the clock face).** For sharp total laws the effective time algebra takes exactly the three shapes of the power-collapse trichotomy: **saturating** — a halting world has finitely many powers with the last absorbing, and $T_{f}$ is finite; **torsion** — a wrapping world acts through an eventually-periodic quotient of $T$, and a fully periodic world ($f^{p} = \mathrm{id}$) acts through the group quantale $\mathcal{P}(\mathbb{Z}/p)$ under Minkowski sum mod $p$ — 4.3's "time wraps onto a circle," now literally a change of scalars; **idempotent** — a thick scale-free step ($f^{2} = f$) collapses every positive scalar to one. AC is the freeness clause (4.3): all powers distinct, or the world halts. *Proof.* Read 4.3's three cases through the morphism: equalities among powers are exactly the relations its image imposes. $\square$ **The dictionary.** Submodules are the induced subtheories of 2.9 — closed under joins and the action, i.e. under $f$: traps and cones (1.9–1.10). Quotient modules are the epi face of observation (2.7; the mono/epi pair of Ch. 15). Module maps are the bare morphisms (categorical interlude §1), the skeleton of simulations (15.1). And **direct products are exactly no-interference**: 13.3 — SUTVA $\iff$ the population arena is a product of unit arenas — reads verbatim as *the population module splits as a direct product of unit modules*; interference is the failure of the splitting. Outside the commutative fiber $T_{f}$ lies everything that is not waiting — other mechanisms, surgeries (12.1) — and that boundary tracks the logical one: the bare one-law theory is decidable, the experimental theory is not (G.3). *Pure time is tame; intervention is wild* — a signpost, not a theorem.
