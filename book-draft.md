# PART I — THE ELEMENTARY THEORY

## Chapter 1. One Axiom: Events and the Law

*Everything in this chapter is defined before it is used. $\mathbb{N} = \{0, 1, 2, \dots\}$. A family in a set $S$ is a function $(A_i)_{i \in I} : I \to S$ from an index set $I$ (possibly empty, possibly uncountable). For a relation $R \subseteq X \times Y$ and $x \in X$, $R(x) := \{y : (x,y) \in R\}$.*

**1.1 Definition (partial order; poset).** A *partial order* on a set $L$ is a relation $\leq\ \subseteq L \times L$ that is reflexive ($A \leq A$), antisymmetric ($A \leq B$ and $B \leq A$ imply $A = B$), and transitive. A *poset* is a pair $(L, \leq)$.

**1.2 Definition (bounds; join; meet).** Let $(L, \leq)$ be a poset and $S \subseteq L$. An *upper bound* of $S$ is $u \in L$ with $A \leq u$ for all $A \in S$. The **join** $\bigvee S$ is the least upper bound: an upper bound below every upper bound (unique by antisymmetry, when it exists). Dually, the **meet** $\bigwedge S$ is the greatest lower bound. For two elements: $A \vee B$, $A \wedge B$.

**1.3 Definition (lattice; complete lattice).** A *lattice* is a poset in which every pair has a join and a meet. A **complete lattice** is a poset in which *every* subset $S \subseteq L$ has a join $\bigvee S$. **Lemma (meets are free).** In a complete lattice every subset also has a meet: $\bigwedge S = \bigvee \{ C \in L : C \leq A \text{ for all } A \in S \}$. *Proof:* that join is a lower bound of $S$ (each $A \in S$ is an upper bound of the set being joined) and is above every lower bound (each lower bound belongs to the set). $\square$ In particular $\bot := \bigvee \varnothing$ (below everything: every element is vacuously an upper bound of $\varnothing$) and $\top := \bigvee L$ exist — **every complete lattice has a zero**. A *nonzero* element is any $A \neq \bot$.

**1.4 Definition (event structure; events; the reading of $\leq$).** An *event structure* is a complete lattice $(L, \leq)$. Its elements are called **events**; $\bot$ is the impossible event, $\top$ the trivial one, nonzero elements the possible events; $A \vee B$ is the disjunctive event, $A \wedge B$ the joint event. The intended reading of $A \leq B$ is *entailment of occurrence*: whenever $A$ occurs, $B$ occurs. **Canonical (pointful) semantics:** take a set $X$ of *states* and $L = \mathcal{P}(X)$ with $\leq\ =\ \subseteq$; "$A$ occurs at state $x$" means $x \in A$; then $A \leq B$ iff every state at which $A$ occurs is a state at which $B$ occurs. The abstract theory keeps $(L, \leq)$ primitive (states optional: Ch. 3); no complements are required — absences are not events.

**1.5 Definition (causal structure — the axiom).** A *causal structure* is a pair $(L, f)$: an event structure and a map $f : L \to L$ such that for **every** index set $I$ and every family $(A_i)_{i \in I}$ in $L$:
$$f\Big( \bigvee_{i \in I} A_i \Big) \;=\; \bigvee_{i \in I} f(A_i).$$
This is the only axiom. Reading: $f(A)$ is the single event "what an occurrence of $A$ brings about next" — possibly a large disjunction, with sub-outcomes recoverable as $f(A) \wedge B$ for any event $B$.

**1.6 Proposition (first consequences).** (a) $f(\bot) = \bot$ — the case $I = \varnothing$ of the axiom: nothing produces nothing. (b) $f$ is monotone: $A \leq B \Rightarrow f(B) = f(A \vee B) = f(A) \vee f(B) \geq f(A)$. (c) Sub-meet law: $f(\bigwedge S) \leq \bigwedge_{A \in S} f(A)$, by monotonicity applied to $\bigwedge S \leq A$. $\square$

**1.7 Examples (first batch).** *(a) The coin.* $X = \{c, h, t\}$; relation $R = \{(c,h), (c,t), (h,h), (t,t)\}$; $L = \mathcal{P}(X)$; $f(A) := R[A] = \bigcup_{x \in A} R(x)$. Join-preservation: images of unions are unions of images. Here $f(\{c\}) = \{h\} \vee \{t\}$: one output event that is a disjunction. "Did the flip possibly cause heads?" — $f(\{c\}) \wedge \{h\} = \{h\} \neq \bot$: yes. "Did it guarantee heads?" — $f(\{c\}) \leq \{h\}$: no. *(b) Translation.* $X = \mathbb{Z}$, $f(\{n\}) = \{n+1\}$ extended by joins. Nothing ever returns. *(c) The 24-hour clock.* $X = \{0, 1, \dots, 23\}$, $f(\{t\}) = \{t + 1 \bmod 24\}$. Every state returns after 24 steps.

**1.8 Definition (walks).** $f^0 := \mathrm{id}$, $f^{n+1} := f \circ f^n$ ($n \in \mathbb{N}$). A *walk* of length $n$ from $A$ is $A, f(A), \dots, f^n(A)$: $n$ applications of the law. Walks are finite by construction: every position is entered by an application from the previous one; a transfinite position would be a limit, which no application enters (limits collect, only successors produce).

**1.9 Definition (forward-closed; reachability).** $Z \in L$ is **forward-closed** if $f(Z) \leq Z$. The **reachability closure** of $A$ is
$$f^{\ast}(A) \;:=\; \bigwedge \{\, Z \in L : A \leq Z \ \text{and} \ f(Z) \leq Z \,\}$$
— the smallest forward-closed event containing $A$. Well-defined: the family is nonempty ($\top$ is forward-closed) and meets exist (1.3). No steps, no $\mathbb{N}$, no induction appear in this definition.

**1.10 Remark (forward-closure is *not* cyclicity).** Read $f(Z) \leq Z$ aloud: *everything $Z$ produces still satisfies $Z$* — the region $Z$ is a trap; futures that enter it stay in it. That is **persistence of a region**, an inequality of containment. A **cycle** is a different thing: an exact *return*, $f^n(x) = x$ for a state, $n \geq 1$ (Ch. 4). Three separations: (i) in translation (1.7b), $Z = \{n : n \geq 5\}$ is forward-closed with $f(Z) = \{n \geq 6\} \lneq Z$, while nothing in that world ever recurs — traps without cycles; (ii) $\top$ is forward-closed in *every* causal structure ("something is the case" persists), which is exactly why the family in 1.9 is nonempty — and is obviously no cycle; (iii) in the clock (1.7c), a pure cycle, the *only* forward-closed events are $\bot$ and $\top$ — a rotation has no proper traps. So cycles do not create forward-closed sets, and forward-closed sets do not witness cycles; everyday instance: "the message has been sent" is forward-closed (once sent, stays sent) in a world where no state ever repeats.

**1.11 Lemma.** $f^{\ast}$ is a closure operator (extensive, monotone, idempotent), and $f^{\ast}(A)$ is itself forward-closed. *Proof:* App. C.

**1.11′ Definition (the induced causal preorder).** Two orders now live on one lattice. The lattice's own $\leq$ (1.1–1.4) is the *logical* order — entailment at a moment; it is, in particular, already a preorder (every partial order is reflexive and transitive; antisymmetry is the extra clause). The **causal** order is not given; it is *induced from $f$*: for $A, B \in L$,
$$A \sqsubseteq B \;:\iff\; B \leq f^{\ast}(A)$$
— "$B$ lies wholly within $A$'s future cone." **Proposition.** *(a) $\sqsubseteq$ is a preorder: reflexive by extensivity ($A \leq f^{\ast}A$), transitive because $C \leq f^{\ast}(B) \leq f^{\ast}(f^{\ast}(A)) = f^{\ast}(A)$ (monotonicity and idempotence of $f^{\ast}$, Lemma 1.11). (b) It is genuinely a preorder, not a partial order: antisymmetry fails — in translation (1.7b), $\{0\}$ and $f^{\ast}(\{0\})$ are mutually $\sqsubseteq$-related yet distinct; in the clock (1.7c), $\{8\} \sqsubseteq \{9\}$ and $\{9\} \sqsubseteq \{8\}$ — on atoms, failures of antisymmetry are exactly the cycles (Axiom AC, Ch. 4, is antisymmetry of $\sqsubseteq$ restricted to atoms). (c) The law advances and preserves it: $A \sqsubseteq f(A)$, and $A \sqsubseteq B \Rightarrow f(A) \sqsubseteq f(B)$.* $\square$ **Honesty clause (why this, and not overlap).** The other natural candidate — $f^{\ast}(A) \wedge B \neq \bot$, "some part of $B$ is reachable from $A$" — is **not transitive** at event level: with $f(a) = b_1$, $f(b_2) = c$ (and $b_1, c$ fixed), $B = \{b_1, b_2\}$, the event $\{a\}$ reaches part of $B$ and part of $B$ reaches $\{c\}$, yet $\{a\}$ never reaches $\{c\}$ — the part of $B$ you can reach is not the part that travels on. Coarse events do not relay. On *atoms* the two candidates coincide (an atom meets an event iff it lies below it, 3.1) and become state reachability $\rightsquigarrow$ (Ch. 4). **Dependency note.** This item could not have come earlier: its preorder-hood is proved from monotonicity (1.6b) via the closure lemma (1.11), so the order of presentation — axiom $\to$ monotonicity $\to$ closure $\to$ induced preorder — is forced by the proofs, not by taste. One lattice, two orders: $\leq$ is what $A$ *says*; $\sqsubseteq$ is what $A$ *sets in motion*.

**1.12 Definition (transfinite stages).** For every ordinal: $Z_0 := A$; $Z_{\alpha+1} := Z_\alpha \vee f(Z_\alpha)$; $Z_\lambda := \bigvee_{\alpha < \lambda} Z_\alpha$ at limits. Uncountable iteration is hereby fully permitted, so its impossibility can be a theorem. (Transfinite induction — "take the least failing stage" — is valid at every cardinality because ordinals are well-ordered; what one cannot induct along is a *dense* order like $\mathbb{R}$, where failures need have no least member.)

**1.13 Theorem (Collapse).** *In every complete lattice, of every cardinality: $Z_\alpha = Z_\omega$ for all $\alpha \geq \omega$.* *Proof:* $f(Z_\omega) = \bigvee_{n} f(Z_n) \leq \bigvee_n Z_{n+1} \leq Z_\omega$ — the axiom, used once; so $Z_{\omega+1} = Z_\omega$, and induction upward (successors add the same nothing; limits join constants). $\square$ Large lattices contain strictly increasing uncountable chains; a join-preserving law climbs at most $\omega$ rungs of any of them.

**1.14 Theorem (Coincidence).** $f^{\ast}(A) = \bigvee_{n < \omega} f^{n}(A) = Z_\alpha$ ($\alpha \geq \omega$); the first equality uses only ordinary $\mathbb{N}$-induction. *Proof:* App. C. **Corollary.** The countable formula is a *theorem about* reachability, not its definition.

**1.15 Example (necessity of the axiom).** $L = [0, \omega_1]$ (ordinals to the first uncountable; a complete lattice), $f(\alpha) = \alpha + 1$, capped at the top. Monotone, **not** join-preserving: $f(\bigvee_n n) = \omega + 1 > \omega = \bigvee_n f(n)$. The stages climb strictly through *all* countable ordinals: genuine uncountable iteration, the moment the axiom is dropped. Reading: this "law" produces from a *completed infinity* — the $\omega$-rule. Credits: Knaster–Tarski vs. Kleene [verify].

**1.16 Definition (production; latency).** For all events $A, B \in L$ and $n \in \mathbb{N}$ with $n \geq 1$: $A$ **produces** $B$ *at latency* $n$ iff $f^{n}(A) \wedge B \neq \bot$ (the joint event "$B$ together with the $n$-step output of $A$" is possible). $A$ **produces** $B$ iff this holds for some finite $n \geq 1$; the **latency** is $\tau(A, B) := \min \{ n \geq 1 : f^n(A) \wedge B \neq \bot \} \in \mathbb{N} \cup \{\infty\}$. The $n = 0$ term ($f^0 = \mathrm{id}$) is excluded by convention: it is the unit — self-connection by doing nothing — quarantined so the diagonal carries information (Ch. 6). Finiteness of $n$ is theorem-backed compression (1.13–1.14), not a restriction. Production is the *overlap* relation of 1.11′ (restricted to $n \geq 1$), and is deliberately not required to be transitive at event level — coarse events do not relay; the composable causal order is $\sqsubseteq$, and the two coincide on atoms.

**1.17 Example (self-producing events, in reality).** In the clock (1.7c): $f^{24}(\{8\}) = \{8\}$, so the event "$8$ o'clock" produces *itself* at latency $24$ — "today's 8 a.m. causes tomorrow's 8 a.m." Nothing runs backwards: the walk goes strictly forward through $9, 10, \dots, 7$ and arrives at a **new occurrence** of the same description. Self-causation in reality is always this: a *type* recurring across distinct *tokens*. The next definition makes tokens rigorous.

**1.18 Definition + Theorem (the unrolled and history structures).** Given a sharp $(X, f)$ (relational case analogous): the **unrolling** is $\hat X := X \times \mathbb{N}$ with $\hat f(x, t) := (f(x), t+1)$ and $\hat L = \mathcal{P}(\hat X)$; the token "type $A$ at time $t$" is $A \times \{t\}$. The **history structure** has states the finite runs $h = (x_0, \dots, x_t)$ with each $x_{i+1} = f(x_i)$, and law $F(h) := h ^\frown f(x_t)$ — the whole past carried along. **Theorem.** (i) Both are causal structures (functions; images preserve joins). (ii) Both satisfy strict acyclicity *for free*: the grade $t$ (resp. length) strictly increases, so no token ever recurs — token acyclicity is a theorem in every causal structure. (iii) $\pi(x, t) = x$ is a simulation ($\pi \circ \hat f = f \circ \pi$), and $x \rightsquigarrow y$ in $X$ iff $(x, 0) \rightsquigarrow (y, n)$ in $\hat X$ for some $n$: type-level causation is exactly the $\pi$-shadow. (iv) $(X, f)$ is recovered by forgetting time (quotient by the shift $(x,t) \mapsto (x, t{+}1)$); for sharp laws with a fixed start, histories and time-stamps carry the same information, while for relational laws histories additionally remember the chosen branch. $\square$ So: *include time for token rigor, or remove it for simplicity — equivalent for every type-level causal fact.* The clock unrolls into an infinite forward helix; rolling the helix back up gives the 24-cycle.

**1.19 Remark (chains / walks / stages) + Proposition (Realization).** A *chain* is a family of events linearly ordered by production; chains may be uncountable and dense. Walks are finite (1.8); stages freeze at $\omega$ (1.13). And chains are cheap: *every* strict order $(P, <)$ is an exact production order with all witnesses at latency $1$ — take $f(A) = \{ s : \exists t \in A,\ t < s \}$. $\square$ A flight over a continuum is one hop; density forbids only an *atomic generator* (Ch. 4), never reachability.

**1.20 Lemma.** Well-ordered subsets of $\mathbb{R}$ are countable: successor gaps swallow distinct rationals. (Separability, not compactness.)

**1.21 Remark (where the finiteness is purchased — the compactness question).** Why is "reachable" exhausted by finitely many steps, when finiteness phenomena usually cost something — in topology, a *compact* space is needed before every cover reduces to a finite subcover? Answer: it is **not free here either; it is bought, by the one axiom — and the purchase is placed on the map, not the space.** Three pieces. *(i) The lattice has no finiteness.* Lattice theory has the user's exact notion under the same name: an element $c$ is **compact** if $c \leq \bigvee D$ for directed $D$ implies $c \leq d$ for some $d \in D$ — finite-subcover, verbatim (topological compactness of a space *is* compactness of $\top$ in its open-set lattice). We assume nothing of the sort: infinite subsets of $X$ are non-compact elements of $\mathcal{P}(X)$, and uncountable chains live in our lattices happily (1.19). *(ii) The axiom is finitariness of the law.* Join-preservation gives, in particular, $f(\bigvee D) = \bigvee f(D)$ for directed $D$ (Scott continuity): the law's output at a completed limit is exhausted by its outputs at the approximants — the law never extracts new information from a finished infinity. That single property is what freezes stage $\omega$ in 1.13; and 1.15 shows it is exactly the purchase price: drop it, keep monotonicity, and iteration genuinely runs to $\omega_1$. *(iii) Topology pays the price we are exempted from.* The closure operator of a topological space, generated by "add all limits of sequences," is monotone but not join-preserving — and iterating it to reach the sequential closure can genuinely require up to $\omega_1$ stages [verify]. Same lattice ($\mathcal{P}(X)$), different operator, transfinite bill. Add the conceptual half — arriving anywhere needs a *last* step, and limits have none (1.8) — and the slogan follows: **don't compactify the space of events; the axiom has compactified the act of producing.**

**1.22 Design commitments (preview of Ch. 22).** Absences cause nothing; mechanism over behavior (intensionality); measures late and once; facts relative to declared structure.

## Chapter 2. Abduction: The Free Right Adjoint

**2.1 Theorem (abduction exists, uniquely).** *Every join-preserving $f$ has a right adjoint $f^{\sharp}(B) := \bigvee\{C : f(C) \leq B\}$, characterized by $f(C) \leq B \iff C \leq f^{\sharp}(B)$, and it is unique.* *Proof:* $f(f^{\sharp}B) = \bigvee\{f(C) : f(C) \leq B\} \leq B$ by the axiom; so $C \leq f^{\sharp}B \Rightarrow f(C) \leq f(f^{\sharp}B) \leq B$; the converse is membership in the defining family; adjoints are unique. $\square$ Reading: $f^{\sharp}(B)$ is **the largest cause of $B$** — the maximal event guaranteed to produce within $B$. Explainability is not postulated; it is the adjoint shadow of production.

**2.2 Theorem (explanation is logical).** *$f^{\sharp}$ preserves arbitrary meets.* *Proof:* $C \leq f^{\sharp}(\bigwedge B_i) \iff f(C) \leq \bigwedge B_i \iff \forall i\, f(C) \leq B_i \iff \forall i\, C \leq f^{\sharp}(B_i) \iff C \leq \bigwedge f^{\sharp}(B_i)$. $\square$

**2.3 Notation ($R$ and $f$ determine each other).** A relation on $X$ is $R \subseteq X \times X$, read causally: $(x,y) \in R$ means "$y$ is a possible immediate successor of $x$"; successor sets $R(x)$; image $R[A] = \bigcup_{x \in A} R(x)$. Dictionary, one line each way: $f_R(A) := R[A]$ preserves joins; conversely $R_f(x) := f(\{x\})$ — **the relation is the law read on atoms** — and join-preservation forces $f(A) = \bigcup_{x \in A} f(\{x\}) = R_f[A]$, with $R_f$ the unique relation presenting $f$ (test on singletons). **Sharpness** (state-determinism): $|R(x)| = 1$; then $R(x) = \{f(x)\}$ recovers the state map, resolving the overloading $f(\{x\}) = \{f(x)\}$.

**2.4 Example (the coin) + the two explainers.** For 1.7(a): $f^{\sharp}(B) = \{x : R(x) \subseteq B\}$, so $f^{\sharp}\{h\} = \{h\}$, $f^{\sharp}\{t\} = \{t\}$, but $f^{\sharp}(\{h\} \vee \{t\}) = \top \ni c$: **certainty of a disjunction does not split** — $f^{\sharp}$ does not preserve joins. Relational abduction splits: $\Diamond B := \{x : R(x) \cap B \neq \varnothing\}$ (possible cause; preserves joins, breaks meets) vs. $\Box B := f^{\sharp}(B)$ (necessary cause; preserves meets, breaks joins). **Theorem:** $\Diamond = \Box$ on all events iff $R$ is total and functional — *sharpness is exactly the collapse of possible and necessary causation*. *Proof:* a fiber with two elements is split by a separating $B$; an empty fiber puts $x$ in $\Box\bot$; singletons identify the two. $\square$

**2.5 Remark (determinism wears three hats — only one is optional).** (i) *The law of events is a function, always*: $f(A)$ is uniquely determined — "if it rains, a determinate set of events happens" is the definition, not an axiom. (ii) *Sharpness* is the optional claim: maximal descriptions stay maximal; its failure is **branching** — a menu of alternatives, no weights, a possibilistic not probabilistic notion. At the atom level multiplicity can only be disjunctive (distinct maximal descriptions exclude each other); the conjunctive multiplicity of "the ground gets wet *and* it cools" is description overlap, present even when sharp. (iii) *Stochasticity does not exist at this level at all* — weights are Part III structure.

**2.6 Proposition (determinization, both directions).** (a) Every $(L, f)$ *is* a deterministic arena: states $= L$, law $= f$ — a relational arena is the atom-level shadow of $(\mathcal{P}(X), R[\cdot])$ (the subset construction): **nondeterminism is the determinism of regions**. (b) Every relational law is the projection of a sharp law on $X \times U$ (a hidden branch-selecting coordinate) — the move unit-resolution makes systematic (Ch. 8). $\square$

**2.7 Observability.** A *read-interface* is a designated subframe $\mathcal{O} \subseteq L$ (closed under arbitrary joins and finite meets; $\bot, \top \in \mathcal{O}$), or dually a quotient $L \twoheadrightarrow L_{\mathrm{obs}}$ — sub vs. quotient is the mono/epi pair of Ch. 12. **Axiom OBS:** $f^{\sharp}(\mathcal{O}) \subseteq \mathcal{O}$ — explanations of observables are observable. **Theorem (continuity, located):** in the sharp powerset case $f^{\sharp} = f^{-1}$ (the adjoint of direct image is preimage), so OBS *is* the historical $\mathcal{O}$-continuity axiom. **Counterexample (do not require forward closure):** $X = \{a, b\}$, $\mathcal{O} = \{\varnothing, \{a\}, X\}$, $f \equiv b$: OBS holds, yet $f(\{a\}) = \{b\} \notin \mathcal{O}$ — a visible cause with hidden effect. The historical $(X, \mathcal{O}, f)$ decomposes as world $+$ read-interface $+$ their compatibility.

**2.8 The instrument panel.** Each candidate preservation law is the axiom, a theorem, or *equivalent to a world property*:

| law | status / world property | witness |
|---|---|---|
| joins forward | **axiom** | — |
| $f(\bot) = \bot$ | theorem (production stance) | — |
| meets backward ($f^{\sharp}$) | theorem (adjointness, 2.2) | — |
| meets forward | $\iff$ injectivity (no merging) | $f[\{2\} \wedge \{9\}] = \bot$, $f[\{2\}] \wedge f[\{9\}] = \{4\}$ (squaring, Ch. 21) |
| joins backward ($f^{\sharp}$) | $\iff$ sharpness | the coin (2.4) |
| $f(\sim A) \leq \sim f(A)$ | $\iff$ injectivity | as above |
| $\sim f(A) \leq f(\sim A)$ | $\iff$ no uncaused states | Garden-of-Eden states are the obstruction |
| both complement laws | $\iff$ **reversibility** | the arrow of time as a failed equation |

$f_{\flat}(B) := \bigvee\{C : f^{\sharp}(C) \leq B\}$: in the sharp powerset case $\{y : f^{-1}(\{y\}) \subseteq B\}$ — the largest event *all of whose causes* lie in $B$ (Garden-of-Eden states belong vacuously). Sharp abduction sits in the adjoint triple $f \dashv f^{\sharp} \dashv f_{\flat}$ — a left and right adjoint at once, which is *why* deterministic $f^{-1}$ preserved every Boolean operation.

**2.9 Induced subtheories.** $M \subseteq L$ closed under $L$-joins with $f(M) \subseteq M$ gives a causal structure $(M, f|_M)$. Examples: (i) every future cone $\downarrow f^{\ast}(A)$ — "the world after $A$" ($f(B) \leq f(f^{\ast}A) \leq f^{\ast}A$); (ii) state-level forward-invariant regions — basins, the recurrent core; the shrinking possibility chain $f^t[X]$ is a *descending chain of subtheories*; (iii) $\mathcal{O}$ under OBS is backward-invariant, so what observation carries is the *abduction* dynamics $(\mathcal{O}, f^{\sharp})$, not production. Honesty: meets in $M$ may differ from meets in $L$ (cf. 3.3).

## Chapter 3. States and the Equivalence Theorems

**3.1 Definition (atoms; states).** An *atom* is a minimal nonzero event. **Lemma:** an atom decides every event: $\alpha \wedge B$ is below $\alpha$, hence $\bot$ or $\alpha$, i.e. $\alpha \wedge B = \bot$ or $\alpha \leq B$. $\square$ $L$ is *atomic* if every element is a join of atoms. **States are the atoms, when there are any** — maximal descriptions; an event is the kind of thing that *happens*, a state the kind of thing that *is*. There are complete lattices with no atoms (regular-open algebras): causality without states, clause (c) below.

**3.2 Theorem (equivalence, three clauses).** *(a)* Sharp arenas $(X, f)$ correspond exactly to causal structures with $L$ a complete atomic Boolean algebra and $f$ atom-preserving: $X = $ atoms, $f(x) = $ the atom $f(\{x\})$, and join-preservation determines $f$ from its atom values. *(b)* Dropping atom-preservation: join-preserving endomaps of $\mathcal{P}(X)$ $=$ relations (2.3). *(c)* The historical coarse $(X, \mathcal{O}, f)$ with continuity corresponds to a frame $\mathcal{O}$ with a frame endomorphism ($= f^{-1}$, preserving arbitrary joins and finite meets) — the *backward* presentation; when $\mathcal{O}$ is spatial, Stone-type duality recovers a state map [verify: sobriety]; beyond spatial frames, the lattice formulation is strictly more general. *Proofs:* App. C.

**3.3 The twist, and an honesty clause.** Nothing runs backwards in the old definition's *world* — its $f : X \to X$ is forward, and clause (a) matches it forward-to-forward. The backwardness lives only in its coarse event-level shadow: continuity protects $f^{-1}$, and the forward image can exit $\mathcal{O}$ (2.7's counterexample) — the historical definition is the right-adjoint face, the elementary one the left-adjoint face, with the adjunction $f \dashv f^{\sharp}$ as dictionary. Honesty: in a coarse frame the *infinite* meet is the largest witnessable event inside the intersection, not the intersection — positivity appearing inside the lattice operations.

**3.4 Example (grain-relative recurrence: the circle).** $X = [0, 2\pi)$, $f(\{x\}) = \{x + 1 \bmod 2\pi\}$. No atom ever returns ($n = 2\pi k$ has no integer solution), so AC holds — genuinely: this world contains no recurrence. Yet the orbit teases: within $0.0177$ of the start at step $44$, within $0.00006$ at step $710$ (the convergent $355/113$) — close forever, equal never. And coarse events recur: the arc $[0, 0.1)$ satisfies $f^n(A) \wedge A \neq \bot$ infinitely often, and $\top$ is invariant. **Sharp descriptions never come home; loose descriptions come home forever.** A finite-precision observer can report recurrence the world does not have — recurrence is relative to grain.

## Chapter 4. Order: Cycles, AC, and Reconstruction

**4.1.** Reachability $x \rightsquigarrow y$ ($\exists n\, f^n(x) = y$) is always a preorder — the atomic face of the induced causal preorder $\sqsubseteq$ (1.11′); the *condensation* (quotient by mutual reachability) is always a poset: causality always induces order, with feedback classes collapsed.

**4.2 Axiom AC; status; price.** **AC:** $\rightsquigarrow$ is antisymmetric — no cycles of length $\geq 2$; fixed points permitted. **AC$_1$ (strict):** no periodic points at all ($f^n(x) \neq x$, all $n \geq 1$). **Proposition.** (a) AC is independent: the 2-cycle models its negation. (b) AC is a *theorem* in three regimes: token unrollings (1.18ii); *graded* laws — any rank $r$ with $r(f(x)) > r(x)$ unless $f(x) = x$ forces antisymmetry (mutual reachability of $x \neq y$ gives $r(x) < r(y) < r(x)$); divisible time (App. A). (c) Price: on finite $X$ with total $f$, orbits repeat (pigeonhole), so the recurrent core is nonempty — AC$_1$ forces $X$ infinite: the *asymmetry–infinity theorem*. $\square$

**4.3 Theorem (power-collapse trichotomy; AC as freeness of time).** Relations among the powers of a law come in exactly three kinds: **halting** ($f^{N+1} = f^N$ — the world settles on fixed points; AC-compatible); **wrapping** ($f^{N+p} = f^N$, $p \geq 2$ — genuine periodicity, what AC forbids: a fully periodic world has $f^p = \mathrm{id}$, the $\mathbb{N}$-action factoring through $\mathbb{Z}/p$ — *time wraps onto a circle*); **scale-freeness** ($f^2 = f$ for thick laws — the dense future operator $R(x) = (x, \infty)$, idempotent yet strictly acyclic; idempotence $\iff$ density of the order). *For sharp total laws, AC holds iff the powers are all distinct or the world halts.* *Proof:* if $f^m = f^n$, $m < n$, every $y = f^m(x)$ satisfies $f^{n-m}(y) = y$, so $y$ is periodic; AC kills periods $\geq 2$, leaving fixed points, so $f^{m+1} = f^m$. $\square$ **AC $=$ the time-action stays free; cycles $=$ time acquires relations.** (The scale-free escape uses no orbit point — thick steps contain their own composites.)

**4.4 Theorem (no token self-causation; torsion of time).** Tokens are occurrences $(A, t)$; production maps $(A, t) \to (B, t+n)$, $n \geq 1$; $(A,t)$ caused by itself forces $t + n = t$, so $n = 0$ — contradiction. $\square$ The proof uses only that the time monoid has no torsion ($t + n = t \Rightarrow n = 0$): over circular time $\mathbb{Z}/p$ token self-causation lawfully returns. The full ledger: *backward causation* — inexpressible (the signature has no negative-latency operation); *latency-0 self-causation* — excluded by definition (the unit); *type-level self-causation* — legitimate (1.17) unless AC$_1$ is assumed.

**4.5 Theorem (no cycles of any length; the scheme).** Define a *cycle*: a finite closed walk $x_0 \to \cdots \to x_k = x_0$ through at least two distinct states. *AC $\iff$ no cycles, uniformly in length.* *Proof:* mutual reachability of $x \neq y$ concatenates to a closed walk; conversely a cycle's prefix and suffix give mutual reachability. $\square$ **Finitariness lemma:** cycles are finitary objects — the only self-production facts are $x \in R^n(x)$, finite $n$ (Collapse: limits add nothing; limits are approached, never produced) — so "no finite cycles" *is* "no cycles." **Non-finite-axiomatizability:** AC is an infinite scheme (one condition per length), provably incompressible — a single $(N{+}1)$-cycle satisfies "no cycles $\leq N$" and defeats any finite axiom set (ultraproduct argument, App. C). Each condition checkable, none redundant: the exact logical cost of total cycle-removal.

**4.6 Theorem (order reconstruction) + Construction + visualization.** A poset is the causal order of: *(a)* a **sharp** structure iff every non-maximal element has a *unique* upper cover, the order is generated by its covering relation (no limit relations), and maximal elements are fixed points — and then the covering relation *is* the law ($\mathrm{covers}(x) = \{f(x)\}$ when $f(x) \neq x$; along one orbit acyclicity forces injectivity, so nothing lies strictly between $x$ and $f(x)$), principal up-sets are chains of type $\leq \omega$, merging appears as shared covers: **the Hasse diagram is the functional graph**; *(b)* a **relational** structure iff cover-generated — including every locally finite poset, i.e. exactly the objects of causal set theory (branching futures $=$ nondeterminism: the diamond needs a relational law); *(c)* **no** step-generated structure when dense (no covering pairs) — continuous time, met from the order side. *Proof sketch:* App. C. **Construction:** given a cover-generated poset, take the law $=$ the covering relation; reachability rebuilds $\leq$; unique covers $+$ maximals-fixed sharpens to a function. **Determinism $=$ unique cover; nondeterminism $=$ branching; density $=$ continuous time.** Visualization: under AC $+$ discreteness, Hasse diagrams *are* the pictures of causal worlds; without AC, draw the condensation and decorate classes with their cycles ($\rho$-portraits, 5.3); dense — no Hasse diagram exists, and the geometry of Ch. 5 takes over.

## Chapter 5. Geometry: One Matrix, Three Semirings

**5.1 The latency quasi-metric.** $d(x, y) := \min\{n : f^n(x) = y\} \in \mathbb{N} \cup \{\infty\}$. Then $d(x,x) = 0$; $d(x,z) \leq d(x,y) + d(y,z)$ (concatenate runs); and asymmetry is generic — $d(x,y) < \infty = d(y,x)$ — *the asymmetry of causation realized as asymmetry of distance*. Future cones are the balls; orbit segments are the geodesics (the only paths the law affords, automatically shortest). **Recovery:** the law is the unit sphere — $f(x)$ is the unique point at distance exactly $1$ (sharp case under AC). $\square$

**5.2 Theorem (one matrix, three semirings).** Encode the law as a matrix $A$ indexed by states; the Kleene star $A^{\ast} = \bigvee_n A^{n}$ computes, per semiring:

| semiring | $A_{xy}$ | $A^{\ast}$ |
|---|---|---|
| Boolean $(\vee, \wedge)$ | $1$ iff $y = f(x)$ | reachability $\rightsquigarrow$ |
| tropical $(\min, +)$ | $1$ iff $y = f(x)$, else $\infty$ | **the latency $d$** — the geometry |
| affine-stochastic (normalized) | $P(y \mid x)$ | hitting structure — the measure layer |

**5.3 Classification of finite arenas.** Every component of a finite sharp arena is a set of trees grafted onto one cycle ($\rho$-shapes); the multiset of cycle lengths plus the grafted tree shapes is a complete isomorphism invariant — credited combinatorics [verify], causal reading: *recurrence skeleton $+$ transient anatomy $=$ the identity of a finite causal world*. ## Chapter 6. Causality over a Quantale

**6.1 Definition (quantale; $V$-causal space — every object formal).** A *commutative unital quantale* $(V, \leq, \otimes, k)$: a complete lattice; an associative, commutative tensor $\otimes$ with unit $k$, preserving arbitrary joins in each argument. Equivalently: a monoid in sup-lattices; a complete idempotent semiring. $V$ is the **value object**; the theory is parametric in it. A **$V$-causal space** is $d : X \times X \to V$ with $k \preceq d(x,x)$ (self-connection) and $d(x,y) \otimes d(y,z) \preceq d(x,z)$ (concatenation). Instances (order $=$ "stronger"; metric rows reversed):

| $\{\bot,\top\}$ | $\bot \preceq \top$ | $\vee$ | $\wedge$ | $\top$ | $\bot$ | reachable? — **preorders** |

**6.2 Definition + Theorem ($V$-events; $V$-laws; the matrix dictionary).** $V$-events: $\varphi : X \to V$ (tropical events are arrival-time profiles, $[0,1]$-events intensity fields). $V^X$ is a complete lattice pointwise and a $V$-module; its $V$-atoms are $\delta_x$ ($k$ at $x$, $\bot$ elsewhere), and $\varphi = \bigvee_x \varphi(x) \otimes \delta_x$. A **$V$-law** is a $V$-module endomorphism of $V^X$. *$V$-laws correspond bijectively to $V$-matrices via $A(x,y) := f(\delta_x)(y)$ and $f_A(\varphi)(y) = \bigvee_x \varphi(x) \otimes A(x,y)$.* *Proof:* decompose on atoms; equivariance and sup-preservation force the formula. $\square$ The exact $V$-generalization of 2.3: at $V = \mathbf{2}$, matrices are relations.

**6.3 Proposition (the graded battery).** Composition $(A \otimes B)(x,z) = \bigvee_y A(x,y) \otimes B(y,z)$ (best route through an intermediate); $I(x,y) = k$ iff $x = y$; star $A^{\ast} = \bigvee_n A^{\otimes n}$. *(i)* $A^{\ast}(x,y)$ is **graded reachability** — join over paths of edge-grade tensors (tropical: latency; $[0,1]$: strongest attenuation path; capacity: widest bottleneck; routes: *the set of label-sequences* — reachability that remembers how) — and $d := A^{\ast}$ satisfies 6.1's two axioms: Theorem 6.4's free construction, explicit. *(ii)* The future cone of $x$ is the $V$-event $d(x,-)$: a graded region; threshold relations $x \leq_v y :\iff v \preceq d(x,y)$ are transitive exactly at idempotent grades. *(iii)* **Graded abduction by residuals:** $f^{\sharp}(\psi)(x) = \bigwedge_y (A(x,y) \multimap \psi(y))$ — Theorems 2.1–2.2 hold verbatim at every $V$. *(iv)* **Parents do not care about $V$:** the minimum-support theorem (8.2) holds word for word for $V$-valued mechanisms — its proof uses only equality of outputs; only the *answers* change with $V$, never the anatomy. *(v)* The support map $\sigma : V \to \mathbf{2}$ is a quantale morphism when $V$ has no zero-divisors: "every $V$ casts a $\mathbf{2}$-shadow" is a functor. $\square$

**6.4 Theorem (star $=$ reflection) + intensionality + Generated Equivalence.** Spaces are exactly the star-fixed laws ($d = d^{\ast}$) — **Spaces $\subsetneq$ Laws**; the star is the free space on a law (left adjoint to the inclusion), not an isomorphism. (i) *Bookkeeping regime:* laws correspond exactly to generated spaces, via $A \mapsto (A^{\ast}, A)$ and $(d, E) \mapsto E$ — the "$+$ generators" carries precisely what the star forgot. (ii) *Canonical regime — when the space alone suffices:* the generator is *definable* from $d$ under atomicity. **6.5 The continuum.** Two *independent* axes, both measure-free. **Continuous space:** nothing new — $X = \mathbb{R}^n$ is a legitimate state set; pointless description is clause 3.2(c). Space-continuity is the **atomicity-of-$L$** axis. **Continuous time:** the watershed bites — a generator exists iff $V$'s positive part has a least element; divisible $V$ has no atoms, no step, no "$f$." **Flow:** $\{A_t\}_{t \in \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}}$, $A_0 = I$, $A_s \otimes A_t = A_{s+t}$ — Chapman–Kolmogorov over $V$. A law is an $\mathbb{N}$-action (free on one generator); a flow is an $\mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}$-action (divisible, free on nothing): *no quantale crosses the gap*. **Thick steps generate faithfully:** $R(x) = [x, x{+}1)$ has star $=$ the dense order, and flow cycles of period $T$ become law cycles within $\lceil T \rceil$ steps — ordinary AC polices them; graded over $[0,\infty]$ ($A(x,y) = y - x$ on spans $\leq 1$) the star returns exact durations. **Sharp samples cannot:** irrational rotation ($x \mapsto x + 1 \bmod 2\pi$) is arena-isomorphic to translation on $\mathbb{R}$ — both bijections, no periodic points, continuum-many $\mathbb{Z}$-chains — so "sampled from a cyclic flow" is not arena-invariant, hence **not axiomatizable** (the no-go); the rotation's return at time $2\pi$ lives between the ticks. **Roots:** a continuum law has $k$-th roots in the composition monoid ($g^2 = f$, e.g. half-spans); "the next moment exists" $=$ "$f$ has no proper roots" — the law-side dual of ATOM-$V$. **AC$_V$ (skeletality):** mutual $k$-connection implies equality — at Lawvere $V$, the metric separation axiom: acyclicity was its $\mathbf{2}$-shadow. **NO-ZENO** (over declared convergence): limits are approached, never produced.

**6.6 Construction (building continuous time).** Three ways to produce flows. *(C1) From a semiflow:* any family $\varphi_t : X \to X$ with $\varphi_0 = \mathrm{id}$ and $\varphi_s \circ \varphi_t = \varphi_{s+t}$ — e.g. the solution flow of a *declared* vector field, the differential structure of App. A — induces the sharp flow $A_t(x) = \{\varphi_t(x)\}$ and the Lawvere causal space $d(x,y) = \inf\{t : \varphi_t(x) = y\}$. *(C2) Root completion:* a coherent root system $g_0 = f$, $g_{n+1}^{2} = g_n$ defines $A_{k/2^n} := g_n^{\,k}$ (well-defined: $g_{n+1}^{2k} = g_n^{k}$); if the family is monotone in $t$ and left-continuous ($A_t = \bigvee_{d<t} A_d$), then $A_t := \bigvee_{\text{dyadic } d \leq t} A_d$ is a flow on all of $\mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}$. Boundary conventions matter: the **half-open** thick step $R(x) = [x, x{+}1)$ on $\mathbb{R}$ has roots $[x, x{+}2^{-n})$ and completes exactly to the flow $A_t(x) = [x, x{+}t)$; the closed step fails left-continuity at the seam. *(C3) Stochastic flows:* the affine instance — 13.5. Sampling any flow at $t = 1$ returns a law; the flow is not recoverable from the sample (the no-go above).

## Chapter 7. Completeness and Decidability

**7.1 Theorem (Kozen) [verify].** An equation in the signature $(\bot, 1, \vee, ;, {}^{\ast})$ holds in every relational causal structure iff it is derivable from the Kleene algebra axioms; the equational theory is decidable (PSPACE). ## Chapter 8. The Splitting

**8.1 Definition (splitting; derived structure).** A *splitting* of an arena $(X, f)$: surjections $\pi_v : X \to X_v$ ($v \in V$ finite) jointly separating points; **full** if $X \to \prod_v X_v$ is a bijection (then $X = \prod_v X_v$). The one posit of Part II. Derived, no further choices: **mechanisms** $f_v := \pi_v \circ f$; **parents** $\mathrm{pa}(v) := \{v' : f_v \text{ essentially depends on } v'\}$ (two states differing only at $v'$ with different $f_v$); **exogenous coordinates** ($f_v = \pi_v$ — the law copies them); **units** $u \in U := \prod_{\text{exo}} X_v$; the dependency graph; acyclicity as a property; the **solution** $\sigma(u)$ in $\leq |V|$ steps when acyclic.

**8.2 Theorem (parenthood is well-defined — exactly when the splitting is full).** *(a) Full splitting: $\mathrm{pa}(v)$ is the unique minimum support of $f_v$.* *Proof:* factoring — if $x, y$ agree on $\mathrm{pa}(v)$, change the inessential coordinates one at a time (every intermediate state exists by fullness; each change preserves $f_v$), so $f_v(x) = f_v(y)$; minimality — an essential $v' \notin S$ gives a pair agreeing on $S$ with different values. $\square$ *(b) Without fullness it can fail:* on $\{(0,0), (1,1)\} \subseteq \{0,1\}^2$, the map "first coordinate" has minimal supports $\{1\}$ and $\{2\}$ and no minimum — "the parents" do not exist. Consequences: **the DAG is a theorem of (law, splitting)**; the failure mode is the deterministic shadow of *positivity*; and a rich frame restores parenthood *interventionally* (if surgeries can set the other dials freely, essential dependence under intervention is well-defined on constrained naturals) — an interventionist account of parenthood, derived rather than assumed.

**8.3 Underdetermination; autonomy.** The splitting is genuine extra structure: distinct split theories can induce the same arena (the forgetful map is faithful, not full). **Autonomy proposition:** same behavior, different mechanism $\Rightarrow$ interventions distinguish — the elementary instance being 6.4's witness; the measured instance, Ch. 14. Observational equivalence is not causal identity.

## Chapter 9. Frames: The Laboratory

**9.2 Surgeries; derived SWIGs.** *Incoming* surgery ($f_W := w$) destroys the natural value. **Outgoing surgery (node-splitting):** keep $f_W$; precompose every *other* mechanism with "read $w$ in the $W$-slot." **Proposition (the SWIG is a derived graph).** By 8.2 applied to the surgered law: $W$ keeps its incoming edges (its mechanism is untouched) and loses its outgoing ones (rerouted mechanisms are constant in $x_W$) — the node-split picture, derived; the derived graph at a *specific* $w$ can be strictly sparser than the union-over-$w$ template (context-specific independence). $\square$ **Notation:** label random nodes $V(a_V)$ with *ancestral* fixed nodes only (minimal labels — exclusion restrictions automatic); regard $\mathcal{G}(\cdot)$ as a *template*, a graph-valued function on the value domain.

**13.4 Example (Markov chains: a particular case).** A discrete-time Markov chain — a state set with a stochastic matrix $P(x,y)$ — is, read against the book, a **graded law at the affine-stochastic face** (5.2; 6.2's matrix dictionary with normalized rows), carrying the **trivial splitting and the empty frame**. **13.5 Construction (continuous-time Markov chains, derived).** Apply the flow definition (6.5–6.6) at the affine face: a family of stochastic matrices $\{P_t\}_{t \geq 0}$ with $P_0 = I$ and $P_s P_t = P_{s+t}$ — Chapman–Kolmogorov *is* the flow law. This is exactly a continuous-time Markov chain. Two presentations, instancing Generated Equivalence (6.4): *(i) Rate matrix:* a declared $Q$ (off-diagonal rates $\geq 0$, zero row sums) presents the flow as $P_t = e^{tQ}$ (finite case). $Q$ is **differential** structure (App. A) — a derivative, not a member of the family — consistent with the no-atomic-step theorem: divisible time still has no smallest tick. *(ii) Uniformization — the law returns:* for $\lambda \geq \max_x |q_{xx}|$, $\Pi := I + Q/\lambda$ is a *discrete* stochastic matrix, and $P_t = \sum_n e^{-\lambda t} \tfrac{(\lambda t)^n}{n!} \Pi^{\,n}$: **a CTMC is a discrete law run at the ticks of a Poisson clock** — continuous time as law $+$ randomized clock, with exponential holding times as the atom-level face of the flow property. Qualitative shadow: for every $t > 0$, $\mathrm{supp}(P_t)$ is full reachability in the rate graph — the $\mathbf{2}$-shadow of a CTMC is *scale-free*, the dense-future phenomenon of 4.3 [verify: CTMC support dichotomy].

## Chapter 14. SWIG Markov and Modularity

**14.1 Theorem (modularity — derived on both sides).** *Product-noise version:* for $V_i \notin A$, $P(V_i(a){=}v \mid \mathrm{pa}_i(a){=}p) = P(V_i{=}v \mid \mathrm{pa}_i{=}p)$. *Proof:* outgoing surgery elsewhere leaves $f_i$ and $\mu_i$ untouched; the kernel is literally the same object. $\square$ *FFRCISTG strength:* per-setting independence suffices (RR Props. 11, 16 — *they derive it too*, by the evaluate-at-consistency argument). The bases differ — counterfactual-independence postulate there, shared-mechanism construction here — and modularity is primitive **nowhere**.

**14.2 Theorem (SWIG global Markov; completeness).** Under per-setting independence, the joint law of the SWIG solution factorizes according to the SWIG; d-separation — fixed nodes $\tilde a$ in the conditioning set, equivalently deleted (RR Props. 13–14) — implies conditional independence among factual treatments and potential outcomes (RR Thm. 12). Moreover the property is **complete** (RR Thm. 19/Cor. 20): d-connection is always realizable in the model class — twin networks lack this. *Proof:* App. C. Two vindications of the *outgoing* choice: natural-value regimes are definable only because the natural value stays alive; and the construction is recoverable by a counterfactual skeptic via observe-then-intervene on an expanded graph (RR §9).

**14.3 Proposition (cross-world no-go; theory-relative).** *The joint law of $(Y(0), Y(1))$ is identified by no frame over the given theory* — no protocol realizes two surgeries on one unit; witness: response-type recouplings preserving every single-world law. $\square$ Cross-world quantities (natural direct/indirect effects, probabilities of causation) are *ontically determined, epistemically gated*: usable behind declared assumptions (per-unit monotonicity), or after **refining the theory** with new intervenable mechanisms — a different causal theory, lawfully reached.

**15.2 Theorem (the g-formula — extended, static, sequential).** Under 14.2's hypotheses, the joint law of the SWIG solution — natural treatments and potential outcomes together — is the product of the *factual* conditionals with assigned values substituted (extended form, RR (37)); marginalizing the childless random halves:

## Chapter 19. The Proving Arena

**19.1 Definition (the dictionary).** States $=$ deductively closed theories; law $=$ one-step saturation (apply every inference rule once); events $=$ positive assertions ("$\varphi$ is established"); **latency $=$ proof depth; unprovability $=$ absence** — the production stance doing real work: what is never derived simply never occurs. **Proposition:** the saturation law is inflationary ($\Gamma \subseteq f(\Gamma)$), so reachability is antisymmetric — AC is a *theorem* here (4.2b). **Proposition (continuity $=$ finitariness):** the law is join-preserving iff the inference rules are finitary; the $\omega$-rule is the discontinuous law — Example 1.15 in proof-theoretic clothing.

**19.2 Labels and interventions.** Labelled (ATMS-style) arenas track *sets of support*: a theorem with several derivations carries several labels — overdetermination of theorems handled at the token level. Axiom-removal interventions have **one-signed effects**: deleting an axiom never adds theorems (monotone deletion) — a frame whose every surgery moves the world in one direction.

## Chapter 20. Identification as Reverse Mathematics

Reverse mathematics *is* identification theory: which axioms (frames) make which theorems (effects) recoverable — "what must be assumed for this to be derivable" and "what must be observed/intervened for this to be identified" are one question. The completeness landscape of Ch. 7, revisited from inside: the proving arena's equational algebra is the complete positive fragment; its first-order theory the incomplete one. The decidability separation (7.3) reads: causation is simple; experimentation is where undecidability enters. The identification arena of Ch. 23 makes this algorithmic: identified $=$ reachable, hedges $=$ Garden-of-Eden.

## Chapter 21. Determination versus Production

**21.1 Proposition (law-level facts are not events).** Properties of $f$ ("$f$ is injective") hold at no state rather than some — they induce only the trivial events $\bot$ or $\top$: a property of the law is not an occurrence in the world the law governs. $\square$ $f$ *determines* its properties; it does not *produce* them — no occurrence, no time, no latency.

**21.2 Worked arena ($x^2 \bmod 11$).** $X = \mathbb{Z}/11$, $f(x) = x^2$. Fixed points $\{0, 1\}$; the 4-cycle $(3, 9, 4, 5)$ ($3^2 = 9$, $9^2 = 4$, $4^2 = 5$, $5^2 = 3$); Garden-of-Eden states $=$ the non-residues $\{2, 6, 7, 8, 10\}$ (never produced); merging from clause interaction ($x$ and $-x$ collide) — the squaring witness of 2.8 lives here: $f[\{2\} \wedge \{9\}] = \bot$ but $f[\{2\}] \wedge f[\{9\}] = \{4\}$. One small arena exhibiting recurrence skeleton, transient anatomy, uncaused states, and merging at once (5.3 in miniature).

**21.3 Witness asymmetry.** A *proof* is a **fossil**: a dependency record witnessing determination — not a producer. Determination is witnessed by a proof object, timelessly; production is witnessed by an occurrence in a run, datedly. Conflating them is the implication-vs-causation error at the meta-level — the error the program was founded on refusing.

## Chapter 22. Production, Absence, and Contrast
